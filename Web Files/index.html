Our Ideology

Vehicle Emissions
Analysis

Exploring which car features drive CO2 output, from engine size to fuel type and transmission.

Problem Statement

What are the most influential car features that affect CO2 emissions the most?

Justification

This question is being researched to help inform consumers when purchasing a car, as their choices can significantly impact the environment. Many features — engine size, fuel type, fuel consumption, and cylinder count — all affect CO2 emissions. Our research compares these factors to help buyers make more environmentally informed decisions. As climate change worsens, reducing personal emissions matters. This work also serves as an incentive for manufacturers to build cleaner, more fuel-efficient vehicles.

Data Used

Dataset 1

Kaggle — Car CO2 Emission

Sourced from Kaggle, derived from official Canadian government records collected over 7 years (2014–2020). Includes manufacturer, model, engine size, cylinders, transmission, fuel type, and city/highway fuel consumption. Arguably the most reliable dataset used, as all data comes directly from the Canadian government.

Dataset 2

Kaggle — Vehicle Emission Dataset

Also from Kaggle, focusing on how conditions like vehicle age affect emissions. Lists engine size, fuel type, max speed, acceleration, and manufacturer. Used to explore how emissions differ based on age and usage, and to examine whether CO2 increases with engine aging in high-mileage vehicles.

Dataset 3

Climate Trace

Sourced from Climate Trace, an independent greenhouse gas tracking organisation. Pulled from their transportation section and cleaned to retain only cars. Provides detailed specs and emissions-per-litre data, useful for cross-referencing the other datasets.

Our Findings

1. Influence of Engine Size and Cylinders

There is a strong positive correlation between engine specifications and CO2 emissions.

Engine Size — As engine displacement (Litres) increases, CO2 emissions rise significantly. Larger engines require more fuel to operate, leading to higher carbon output.

Cylinders — A similar trend is seen with cylinder count. Vehicles with 8, 10, or 12 cylinders emit substantially more CO2 than those with 3 or 4 cylinders.

2. Impact of Petrol Type

The data reveals a clear difference between the two petrol types.

Regular Gasoline — Average emissions are approximately 235.12 g/km.

Premium Gasoline — Average emissions are higher at approximately 266.04 g/km.

Conclusion — Premium gasoline is generally used in high-performance or luxury vehicles with more power-hungry, less efficient engines, which explains the higher average emissions.

3. Efficiency and Fuel Consumption

Efficiency is the most critical predictor of CO2 emissions.

The relationship between Fuel Consumption (L/100 km) and CO2 Emissions is approximately linear. Every additional litre of fuel consumed per 100 km results in a direct increase in CO2 grams per km.

Conclusion — Improving a vehicle's fuel efficiency (lowering L/100 km) is the most direct way to reduce its carbon footprint.
